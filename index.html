    Intrinsic rewards in reinforcement learning provide a powerful algorithmic capability for agents to learn how to interact with their environment in a task-generic way. We introduce a method for computing an intrinsic reward for curiosity using a measurement of how well agents can perceive their environment. Our agents learn a model of their environment via a conditional variational autoencoder (CVAE). Our measurement of surprise for a given outcome is computed by sampling the latent space of our CVAE and computing an associated conditional probability of the current state given the previous state. For our experiments, a video game agent uses our model to autonomously learn how to play Atari games without extrinsic rewards. We find that our perception module learns to accurately reconstruct scenes from each game. We also show that the policy learned by an agent using our probabilistic formulation of surprise outperforms policies learned with other reward schemes based on heuristics such as pixel-level reconstruction error.

Intrinsic Curiosity Module (ICM)

We defineBayesian surpriseas the amount an agent should be curious about an observation derived from a conditional likelihood of the observation occurring given the current world model of the agent. From our definition of curiosity, we want to reward actions more strongly which result in less likely outcomes. Therefore, we use the negative of this conditional probability estimate as a reward for agents. paper.
